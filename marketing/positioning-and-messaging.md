# Kalas — Positioning & Messaging

**Status:** Working draft · Beta phase · Denmark (Copenhagen-first) · Bilingual EN/DA
**Owner:** Positioning & messaging
**Last updated:** 2026-07-05

Kalas (Swedish for "celebration") is a conversational AI wedding planner. The agent persona is **Ava**. This document is the single source of truth for how we describe Kalas — on the landing page, in ads, in the app, and when a journalist asks "so what is it?"

---

## 1. Positioning statement

> **For engaged couples in Denmark who are drowning in wedding admin but can't justify DKK 30,000+ for a human planner, Kalas is an AI wedding planner that actually does the legwork — Ava finds real venues and vendors, emails them from your own Gmail, chases replies, and lays the quotes out side by side — unlike directories and checklists that hand you a list and leave the thirty email threads to you.**

Shorter, for pitch decks and bios:

> Kalas is the AI wedding planner that does the work, not just the checklist. Tell Ava about your wedding; she finds the vendors, handles the outreach from your own inbox, compares the quotes, and keeps budget, guests, and your wedding website up to date — while every decision stays yours.

**The category call:** we say **"AI wedding planner"** — not "wedding planning app," not "wedding assistant." "App" puts us next to checklists and directories, which we out-do. "Planner" invites comparison with a human, which we win on price and availability and honestly concede on day-of presence (see §4). "Assistant" undersells the agency: Ava sends the emails, chases the vendors, extracts the quotes.

**The one differentiator to protect in all copy:** *outreach happens from the couple's own Gmail, and Ava reads the replies.* No competitor in the Danish market does this. Every piece of messaging should keep this concrete: "she emails thirty venues so you don't have to" beats any abstract claim about AI.

## 2. Ideal customer profiles

Danish market context that shapes all three personas: Danish weddings commonly run DKK 100,000–200,000+; human planners cost DKK 25,000–60,000 and are a niche, "luksus" purchase — most Danish couples DIY. Venue supply around Copenhagen is fragmented (herregårde, forsamlingshuse, restaurants, waterfront venues) with slow, inconsistent email response habits. That gap — *"I won't pay for a planner, but the admin is killing me"* — is our market.

### Persona A: The busy Copenhagen couple ("Sofie & Mikkel") — PRIMARY

- **Who:** 28–38, both working full-time (consulting, tech, healthcare, public sector), living in København/Frederiksberg/Valby. Budget DKK 100–180k, 80–130 guests, wedding 12–18 months out. Planning happens on the sofa on Tuesday nights and gets bumped whenever work is busy.
- **Pains:**
  - Vendor outreach is a part-time job: 25+ inquiry emails, half never answered, follow-ups forgotten.
  - Quotes arrive as unstructured PDFs and prose; comparing them means rebuilding everything in a spreadsheet.
  - One partner carries the mental load and resents it; planning becomes a source of friction, not joy.
  - Popular Copenhagen-area venues book out 12–18 months ahead — slowness has a real cost.
- **Watering holes:** Instagram/TikTok wedding content; Danish Facebook groups ("Bryllupsinspiration," "Bryllup 2026/2027," buy-and-sell wedding groups); Google searches like *"bryllupslokaler København"*; Bryllupsklar and similar directories; bryllupsmesser (wedding fairs, e.g. in Forum/Øksnehallen); recommendations from recently married friends — the single biggest channel.
- **Objections & our answers:**
  - *"An AI sending emails as me? Awkward."* → You approve every email before it goes; it's sent from your Gmail, signed by you, and you see the whole thread.
  - *"Is the venue data real or hallucinated?"* → Ava searches live listings and shows real venues with photos; every quote comes from an actual vendor reply in your inbox.
  - *"499/month is a new subscription."* → It's ~1–2 months of use at the intensive phase, roughly the price of one bouquet — versus DKK 30,000+ for a human planner. Frame as a wedding line item, not a subscription.
- **Message that lands:** time and mental load. "Clear Ava's suggestions on your lunch break."

### Persona B: The destination couple ("Emma & Jacob") — expats or couples abroad marrying in Denmark

- **Who:** One or both partners Danish, living in London/Berlin/New York/Dubai — or an international couple in Copenhagen where one partner doesn't speak Danish. Marrying in Denmark for family, or because Denmark's simple marriage paperwork makes it a genuine destination-wedding country. Often shorter timelines, sometimes higher budgets.
- **Pains:**
  - Can't do site visits or phone calls; time zones make vendor phone-tag impossible — email is their only channel, which is exactly the channel Ava owns.
  - Language: Danish vendor websites, Danish quotes, Danish contract terms. The non-Danish partner is locked out of half the process.
  - No local network — no mother-in-law who "knows a place in Nordsjælland."
  - Coordinating decisions as a couple across two countries.
- **Watering holes:** "Expats in Copenhagen"-type Facebook groups and expat forums; r/Denmark and r/weddingplanning; international planning sites (The Knot, Hitched, Zola) that don't cover Denmark; Google searches in English ("getting married in Denmark," "wedding venues Copenhagen"); destination-wedding agencies (our expensive alternative).
- **Objections & our answers:**
  - *"Can it actually communicate with Danish vendors?"* → Kalas is bilingual — brief Ava in English, she corresponds with vendors in Danish, and you read everything in the language you prefer.
  - *"We can't visit — how do we trust a venue?"* → Real listings with photos, structured quotes with what's included spelled out, and every vendor reply preserved in your own inbox. (Honest limit: we don't replace a site visit; we make the shortlist good enough that one trip home covers it.)
  - *"A destination planner handles legal paperwork too."* → We don't do marriage paperwork (yet); position Kalas as the vendor-and-logistics layer, and be upfront about it.
- **Message that lands:** distance and language. "Plan a Danish wedding from anywhere — Ava speaks Danish so you don't have to."

### Persona C: The budget-first DIY couple ("Freja & Anders")

- **Who:** 25–34, in Copenhagen's suburbs or Aarhus/Odense (our expansion path), budget DKK 60–110k, watching every krone. Proudly DIY — they were always going to plan it themselves; a human planner was never on the table. Currently running the wedding out of Google Sheets, a shared Note, and a groaning inbox.
- **Pains:**
  - Getting *enough quotes to compare* is the whole game on a tight budget — and it's exactly the most tedious part.
  - Fear of overpaying or of hidden costs ("is moms included? is cleanup included?").
  - Budget creep: fifteen small "just 2,000 more" decisions with no running total.
  - The spreadsheet is always stale; the couple's system decays around month four.
- **Watering holes:** Danish Facebook buy/sell and budget-wedding groups; TikTok "bryllup på budget" content; Trustpilot research on vendors; forsamlingshus and DIY-venue searches; Pinterest.
- **Objections & our answers:**
  - *"We can do this ourselves for free."* → Yes — start free. The free tier (venue search, shortlisting, 5 outreach emails, quote comparison) is genuinely useful, and this persona is our free-tier volume and word-of-mouth engine.
  - *"499 kr./md. is real money for us."* → One month of Complete typically pays for itself the first time a side-by-side comparison reveals what's *not* included in the cheap quote. Budget alerts are literally a money-saving feature — lead with them for this persona.
- **Message that lands:** control and money. "More quotes, fewer surprises, and a budget that updates itself."

**Prioritization:** A is the beachhead (density, willingness to pay, Copenhagen-first fit). B is the highest-differentiation segment — bilingual + email-native is almost a category of one, and it's reachable through cheap, concentrated channels. C is free-tier growth and the moat against "we'll just DIY."

## 3. Messaging house

### Core promise (roof)

> **Ava does the wedding admin. You make the decisions.**

Every claim we make must ladder up to one of these two clauses: either it shows Ava *doing real work* (not suggesting, not listing — doing), or it shows the couple *staying in control* (approve, decide, oversee). The existing site already lives this ("She handles the wedding. You stay in charge." / "Ava does it. You oversee it.") — keep that consistency.

### Pillars and proof points

| # | Pillar | What we claim | Proof points (all shipped features) |
|---|--------|---------------|-------------------------------------|
| 1 | **She does the legwork, not the checklist** | Ava executes: searches, emails, follows up, extracts, compares. | Grounded live-web search across venues, caterers, photographers, florists; personalized inquiries sent from *your own Gmail*; automatic reply monitoring (inbox polled every few minutes); Ava chases vendors who go quiet; quotes extracted into a side-by-side comparison of price, availability, and inclusions. |
| 2 | **Nothing happens without you** | You approve every email, every booking step; Ava queues, you clear. | Approval queue on mobile and desktop; one master inquiry you review before anything is sent; drafted replies wait for your go-ahead; emails signed by you, in your voice, visible in your own inbox forever. |
| 3 | **One place from venue to "I do"** | Not just vendor search — the whole wedding stays current in Kalas. | Budget tracks every deposit and commitment and flags overruns before they happen; guest list, RSVPs, meal choices, seating in one place; save-the-dates and invitations sent on approval; wedding website that Ava keeps updated; planning timeline that nudges ("time to book your photographer"). |
| 4 | **Built for Denmark** | This isn't a US product with the dollar sign swapped. | Danish venues and vendors in the search results; prices and budgets in DKK; fully bilingual English/Danish — brief Ava in either, vendors get natural Danish; Copenhagen-first venue coverage; pricing in DKK with a real free tier. |

### Message-by-persona

| Persona | Lead pillar | Second | Lead line to use | Avoid |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **A — Busy Copenhagen couple** | 1 (legwork) | 3 (one place) | "She emails thirty venues so you don't have to — clear her suggestions on your lunch break." | AI-tech framing ("LLM-powered," "agentic"). They buy time back, not technology. |
| **B — Destination / expat** | 4 (built for Denmark) | 1 (legwork) | "Plan a Danish wedding from anywhere. Ava writes to vendors in Danish — you read everything in English." | Assuming Danish cultural context; over-promising (no site visits, no marriage paperwork). |
| **C — Budget-first DIY** | 3 (one place — lead with budget) | 2 (control) | "More quotes, no surprises: Ava compares what's actually included, and your budget updates itself." | Any "replace your planner" framing — they never had one. Position against the spreadsheet, not the planner. |

**Beta note:** while in beta, honesty is a feature. "Kalas is in beta — Copenhagen venues first, more cities as we grow" reads better with Danish audiences (low tolerance for hype, Jantelov-adjacent skepticism of big claims) than pretending to be finished.

## 4. Competitive landscape

Three real alternatives. Be honest — Danish buyers punish overclaiming, and beta credibility depends on it.

### vs. Human wedding planners (DKK 25,000–60,000)

- **Where Kalas wins:** price (~50–100× cheaper for the planning phase); availability (Ava answers at 23:40 on a Tuesday); volume (a human sends 5 hand-picked options; Ava contacts every venue you liked and structures every reply); transparency (all correspondence lives in *your* inbox — no black box, no commission bias toward preferred vendors); the couple stays hands-on, which most Danish couples actually want.
- **Where Kalas honestly doesn't:** taste and curation from years of local relationships; negotiation muscle and vendor favors; **day-of coordination** — Ava will not be there when the florist is late on the morning of the wedding; emotional hand-holding and family diplomacy.
- **How to position:** don't attack planners — almost none of our market was going to hire one anyway. Frame: *"A human planner is wonderful if it's in the budget. For everyone else, there's Ava."* For the high end, Kalas + a day-of coordinator (DKK 5–10k) beats a full-service planner on price and control — a useful line for Persona A's premium slice and the Premium tier.

### vs. DIY (spreadsheets + Google + your inbox)

The real competitor for the Danish market. Everyone starts here.

- **Where Kalas wins:** the outreach grind (write once, Ava personalizes and sends to everyone, then chases non-responders — DIY's single most hated task); structured comparison (quotes extracted automatically vs. re-typed into Sheets); freshness (the DIY spreadsheet is stale by month four; Kalas updates itself as replies land); the mental load (Ava remembers what's next so neither partner has to be the project manager of their own wedding).
- **Where Kalas honestly doesn't:** price (DIY is free; our free tier is the answer); total flexibility (a spreadsheet can model anything); zero trust required (some couples will simply enjoy the control — let them, and let the free tier convert the ones who burn out).
- **How to position:** never mock DIY — validate it. *"You're already the planner. Ava is the assistant you'd hire if assistants cost 499 kroner."* Attack the grind, not the couple's competence.

### vs. Marketplaces & directories (Bryllupsklar-type Danish directories; Zola/The Knot/Hitched as international reference points)

- **Where Kalas wins:** they end where the pain begins — a directory hands you 40 venue links and a contact form; you still write 40 emails, chase 40 threads, and build the comparison yourself. Kalas does that entire second half. Directory contact forms send your details to venues who then market *at* you; Ava works *for* you, from your own address. International players (Zola, The Knot) have effectively zero Danish vendor coverage, no DKK, no Danish — they're a reference point for what the category looks like abroad, not a real competitor here.
- **Where Kalas honestly doesn't:** curated vendor depth and verified reviews (directories have years of accumulated listings and social proof; our live-web search is broad but not curated, and result quality varies); vendor-side relationships (directories are paid by vendors and have their loyalty — noting we have a `/vendors` page and could grow that side); brand trust of an established name vs. a beta.
- **How to position:** complementary, then superior. *"Found a venue on a directory? Paste it in — Ava will contact them too."* Directories are a discovery layer; Kalas is the execution layer. Long-term the honest sentence is: **directories give you a list; Ava gets you answers.**

### Positioning map (one line)

> Human planners are high-touch and expensive. DIY is free and exhausting. Directories show you options and leave you the work. **Kalas is the only option that does the work with you — at software prices.**

## 5. Tagline candidates

### English

| # | Tagline | Notes |
|---|---------|-------|
| E1 | **Plan the wedding. Skip the stress.** *(incumbent)* | Proven on the hero, rhythmic, benefit-clear. Weakness: "skip the stress" is claimed by half the wellness industry; says nothing about *how*. |
| E2 | **You say yes. Ava does the rest.** | Double meaning of "yes" (the vows + the approval flow) is exactly our product truth. Rhymes without being cheesy. |
| E3 | **The wedding planner who answers at midnight.** | Sharp vs. human planners; concrete; great for ads. Too narrow as a permanent tagline. |
| E4 | **All the wedding. None of the admin.** | Clean pain articulation, works for Personas A and C. |
| E5 | **Thirty vendors. One conversation.** | Most concrete expression of the differentiator; strong for performance ads, too feature-level for the brand roof. |
| E6 | **Your wedding, off your plate.** | Warm, mental-load framing; slightly risks implying loss of control (our copy must always pair it with "you decide"). |

### Danish (idiomatic, not translations)

| # | Tagline | Back-translation & notes |
|---|---------|--------------------------|
| D1 | **Sig ja — Ava klarer resten.** | "Say yes — Ava handles the rest." *Sig ja* is literally what you say at the altar in Danish, and it's the approval button in the app. The strongest Danish line we can own. |
| D2 | **Bryllup uden bøvl.** | "A wedding without hassle." *Bøvl* is beautifully colloquial Danish for annoying friction; punchy and very Danish in tone. |
| D3 | **Hun planlægger. I fester.** | "She plans. You (two) celebrate." Clean contrast; *I* (plural you) addresses the couple directly. |
| D4 | **Jeres bryllup. Hendes benarbejde.** | "Your wedding. Her legwork." Mirrors the "you decide / she executes" architecture of the site. |
| D5 | **Drømmebrylluppet — uden regnearket.** | "The dream wedding — without the spreadsheet." Perfect for Persona C and anyone mid-DIY; too narrow for the roof. |
| D6 | **Glæd jer til brylluppet. Også til at planlægge det.** | "Look forward to the wedding. And to planning it, too." *At glæde sig* is a deeply Danish concept (anticipatory joy); warmer, longer, campaign-friendly. |

### Recommendation

- **English: keep E1 "Plan the wedding. Skip the stress."** as the hero line — it's live, it works, and beta is not the moment to rebrand the front door. Adopt **E2 "You say yes. Ava does the rest."** as the secondary line for ads, app store, and social, and let performance data decide whether it earns the hero slot post-beta. Use E5 for cold performance ads where concreteness wins.
- **Danish: D1 "Sig ja — Ava klarer resten."** *Sig ja* fuses the wedding vow with the product's approval mechanic in two words — no English line does that. It's warm, short, ownable, and it encodes pillar 2 (you approve) and pillar 1 (she executes) simultaneously. D2 ("Bryllup uden bøvl.") is the backup and the better fit for budget-focused (Persona C) campaigns.
- The EN/DA pair E2 + D1 are conceptual siblings ("you say yes / sig ja"), which keeps the bilingual brand coherent without literal translation — exactly the standard we should hold all Danish copy to.

## 6. Voice & tone: Ava as a brand voice

Ava is the voice of the product — in chat, in notifications, in marketing. She is **a supremely organized friend who used to plan events for a living**: warm, concrete, unflappable, never gushing, never robotic. Danish register: *hyggelig* but competent — think a great toastmaster, not a salesperson.

**Rule 1 — Show the work, don't announce the magic.** Ava earns trust by being specific about what she did and found, never by praising herself or her AI-ness.
- ✅ *"I found 14 venues near Copenhagen that fit 120 guests under 80k. Three have your date open — want the shortlist?"*
- ❌ *"Great news! My advanced AI has discovered some AMAZING venues for your special day! ✨"*

**Rule 2 — Always hand back the wheel.** Every message that involves action ends with the human's decision point. Ava proposes; the couple disposes. Never imply she acted without approval.
- ✅ *"Here's the inquiry I'd send to all six venues. Edit anything you like — nothing goes out until you approve."*
- ❌ *"I've gone ahead and emailed the venues for you!"*

**Rule 3 — Calm about money, always in kroner.** Budget talk is factual and early — no euphemism, no alarmism, no dollar-thinking. Ava flags overruns before they happen and states trade-offs plainly.
- ✅ *"This caterer quote is DKK 6,400 over your food budget. You could trim the welcome drinks, or I can ask two cheaper caterers for quotes — which do you prefer?"*
- ❌ *"Uh oh! 😬 Looks like the budget is getting a little tight! Don't worry, love finds a way!"*

**Rule 4 — Warm, not gushing.** One wedding-appropriate touch of warmth per message, maximum. No exclamation-mark stacking, no "magical journey," no "big day" clichés, at most one emoji and usually none. Danish audiences read hype as untrustworthy.
- ✅ *"The photographer confirmed — she's yours for October 3rd. One more thing off the list."*
- ❌ *"YAY!!! 🎉💍✨ Your DREAM photographer said YES to your magical day!!!"*

**Rule 5 — Idiomatic in both languages, and honest about limits.** Danish Ava writes natural Danish (*"Skal jeg rykke dem for et svar?"*), never translated English; English Ava never sprinkles Danish for charm. And when Ava doesn't know or can't do something (a vendor hasn't replied, a listing lacks prices, Kalas doesn't handle marriage paperwork), she says so plainly and offers the next step.
- ✅ *"Two venues still haven't answered after my follow-up. Some places only respond by phone — their numbers are here if you want to call, and I'll keep watching the inbox."*
- ❌ *"Don't worry — everything is under control and going perfectly!"*

**Quick litmus test for any new copy:** Would a brilliant, slightly understated Danish wedding planner say this to a couple she likes? If it sounds like a SaaS product or an American greeting card, rewrite it.
